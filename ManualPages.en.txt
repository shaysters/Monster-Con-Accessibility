# Monster Con info manual speech descriptions.
# The game renders the manual pages as sprites, so these entries describe the visible text and artwork.

0=Page 1, The Basics. Text: The goal of Monster Con is to succeed at having the best con afterparty ever. This can be accomplished by scoring a date or going by yourself. By the end of the run, you will choose who to ask for the con afterparty: one of the six Romance Options, or none of them as a seventh option. To get a successful ending you must meet requirements, usually tied to the player's stats. Image: a screenshot of the final choice screen asks "Who will be your con love?" with six character portrait cards, April selected at the bottom, a Day 4 calendar, and a blue stats box listing Smarts 13, Boldness 10, Creativity 9, Charm 13, Fun 6.
1=Page 2, The Basics, What Are Stats? Text: Each player has five stats: Smarts, Boldness, Creativity, Charm, and Fun. Players manage stats through mechanics; to win, they must manage stats strategically and meet good-ending requirements. The page labels the goal as "Achieving a good ending" and the strategy as "Properly managing your stats." Image: the same final choice screen is shown, with a turquoise circle drawn around the top-right stat panel. Five small stat icons are displayed: blue glasses, red sunglasses grin, purple lightbulb face, pink kissing face with heart, and yellow laughing face.
2=Page 3, Types of Good Ending. Text: Monster Con can be won in different ways. Romance Options have unique stat-tied requirements; later pages give hints for those requirements. Secret endings come from doing plotlines and asking the correct person for a date, either one of the six Romance Options or "None of them." Guests have unique requirements, with a hint given the first time you meet them. For going alone, you must be balanced enough not to do too badly in any part of your personality. Image: four labeled panels show ending types: Date one of the 6 main Romance Options, Get one of the Secret Endings, Date one of the Guests, and Get a successful Alone Ending.

6=Page 7, How to Manage Your Stats. Text: "There's a series of ways in which you can manage your stats. Here's a summary of them:" Image: seven screenshot tiles with black captions. Choosing Likes slightly affects your stats. Choosing your Top 3 at the start of every run. Going to a Location gets you stats before an event starts. Event Options are the main way to affect your stats. Sitting by yourself in the Food Court will affect your stats. The Options in the Comic also affect stats. Mystery Shop items can also affect your stats.
7=Page 8, Likes and Top 3, Likes. Text: Before entering the convention, you define your character through Likes and Top 3. During the Likes stage, you choose three likes from twelve options. Each Like is associated with one or several Romance Options, counts as preference or interest points toward a Romance Option, and affects which Romance Options are favored in events. Likes also tie to a specific stat, giving plus one to that stat when building starting stats. Even after the con starts, there are ways to affect interest points and tell the game to focus on a different Romance Option. Image: a Likes screen asks "Tell us 3 things you like," showing twelve rectangular choices such as Idol Choreographies, Wholesome Memes, Heavy Metal Idols, and Garfield x Naruto, with three empty selected slots at the bottom and Yellow as the player label.
8=Page 9, Likes and Top 3, Top 3. Text: During Top 3, you see five things in a category and choose your top three in decreasing order: first preference, second preference, third preference. Each option is tied to one stat, and your chosen order shapes initial stats by increasing the stats tied to your choices. Image: a quiz screen asks "Choose your top 3 favorite Pokemans." Five chibi choices appear with blank selection circles beside them. Blue is the player label at bottom left.
9=Page 10, Locations. Text: During the Con turns or Main turns, you choose one of five locations. Going to a specific location gives access to a pool of events tied to that location. Before an event starts, you see a short standard event tied to the location, which gives plus 2 to a specific stat. Image: an illustrated convention map labels the five locations and their stats: Main Stage equals Smarts, The Lobby equals Boldness, Hotel and Outdoors equals Creativity, Merch Hall equals Charm, and Community Zone equals Fun. A banner says, "Want to increase a specific stat? Choose the correct location for it!"
10=Page 11, Events: Stat Exchange. Text: Events are the main narrative core of the game. Your character faces a conflict and chooses between two options. Choosing an option shows a specific outcome and affects stats through stat exchange. Each option is tied to one stat. Choosing an option gains an amount of that option's stat and loses an amount of the stat tied to the option not chosen. Example text explains a panel scenario where solving a riddle is tied to Smarts and punching someone in the face is tied to Boldness; punching would gain Boldness but lose Smarts. Image: a screenshot shows three characters in an event and two dialogue choices, with a visible stats panel on the right.
11=Page 12, How to Choose Options. Text: The main event mechanic is guessing which stat is associated with each option, so stats can be managed for the good ending. A note says you can ignore this and choose options based on what is funnier or more intriguing, but a stat glossary follows if you want to guess every stat. Image: a screenshot shows Liam and April in an event, with two choices. The top selected red choice says, "No time to think! Smash your arm with your emergency hammer until you finally have something broken with you." The lower black choice starts, "Your bank account. You've been broke for too long..." Two stat arrows at right show the chosen option tied to Boldness and the other to Smarts.
12=Page 13, Stat Glossary. Text: Boldness is anything blunt, reckless, or unnecessarily dangerous; look for illegal-feeling actions or actions involving lots of violence. Smarts is any option about knowing something obscure, being clever, or being cogent. Magic use is tied to Smarts. Arcane summoning gives Smarts. Inventing something that needs more brain than imagination is probably Smarts. Image: two large cream cards on a cyan background, each headed by a stat icon: red sunglasses grin for Boldness and blue glasses grin for Smarts.
13=Page 14, Stat Glossary. Text: Creativity is tied to arts and crafts: singing an aria, writing a sonnet, or sewing a dress. It also covers especially out-of-the-box options, and options referring to high culture such as a well-known painting. Fun is party, sex, and chaos; violent chaos may be Boldness, otherwise it can be Fun. Fun is tied to drugs, earthy pleasures such as food, pranks, jokes, and usually pop culture such as TV shows and videogames. Image: two cream cards with stat icons: a purple face with a lightbulb for Creativity and a yellow laughing face for Fun.
14=Page 15, Stat Glossary. Text: Charm is using people skills, good or evil: persuading, charming, flirting, lying, befriending, and similar actions. Complimenting the person you are trying to romance is probably Charm. Romantic or very sweet actions are Charm. Wearing a beautiful dress is Charm because it is about fashion and looking good, while making the dress might be Creativity. Good deeds or fighting for good can also be Charm. Note: an option can feel tied to more than one stat; look for the biggest clue. Image: one large cream Charm card with a pink kissing-face stat icon and a black note card.
15=Page 16, Food Court Stages. Text: Twice during a game you visit the Food Court, choosing one of five tables. Three tables contain two Romance Options each, one table lets the player sit by themselves, and one table lets the player call a Guest. Only one player can sit at each table on a Food Court turn. Image: a top-down Food Court screenshot with wooden tables labeled A, B, and C in pink circles. Player and character icons stand near tables. A blue stats panel appears at top right.
16=Page 17, Food Court, Romance Options. Text: Choosing one of the three Romance Option tables first asks which of the two Romance Options you want to interact with. Choosing a Romance Option increases your preference toward them, helping the game give you events that include them. You can then choose from several date options; each date shows short text and cute chibi images. Dates differ in two ways. A black note says each Romance Option has two locked dates tied to plotlines; completing a plotline unlocks that date for manual access later. Another note says all dates have a pepper count, from platonic to naughty, and the pepper guide is on the next page. Image: April is selected beside a list of date options: Board Game Night with three peppers selected, Watch a Movie with one pepper, Go for a Walk with one pepper, and locked options Go Out for Lunch and Visit a Museum.
17=Page 18, Food Court, Romance Options, Pepper Count: A Glossary. Text: No peppers means the ending is purely platonic. One pepper means romantic without being naughty. Two peppers means romantic and somewhat suggestive. Three peppers means more on the naughty side. Note: every Romance Option always has No Peppers and Three Peppers options. Pepper count affects endings, not the dates themselves. A Three Peppers date is not necessarily naughty. Pepper count does not affect Secret Endings. Image: pepper icons appear as dotted pepper outlines, one red pepper, two red peppers, and three red peppers beside their definitions.
18=Page 19, Food Court, Romance Options, Conversations. Text: If you visit the same Romance Option twice, once per Food Court turn, the second visit gives access to Conversations. You choose one conversation topic out of three. Each Romance Option has six conversation topics. Conversations can improve chances to score a date, but are mostly for fantasy and flavor, giving deeper access to the Romance Option's life and personality. One Romance Option has a seventh unlockable conversation topic full of lore. Image: a screenshot shows April and a grey-haired character seated at a balcony at night under the prompt "Talk about..." The visible topic choices are "Her streaming channel," "Her past," and "The contract."
19=Page 20, Food Court, Sitting Alone. Text: Sitting by yourself works like sitting with a Romance Option. You get date options. If you go on a date by yourself, you see chibi art and text for the date. Doing this twice accesses a self-conversation, where the playable character reflects. Each playable character has one self-conversation. Some date options are initially locked; unlocking them lets you trigger special plotlines not tied to a specific Romance Option. A black note says regular dates give a slight stat boost, while plotline dates must first be found randomly and then successfully completed before they can be manually triggered again. Image: a screenshot asks "What do you want to do?" with a red-haired player portrait and date choices such as Visit a Museum, Go for a Walk, Watch Some TV, Go for a Lunch, and Play Some Games. Several choices show pepper icons, scroll icons, or padlocks.
20=Page 21, Food Court, Call a Guest. Text: Choosing this table lets you call one of several Guests, who are dating alternatives to Romance Options. More guests unlock as you keep playing. Guests greet you and give a hint about their requirements for their unique ending. Later in the Con stage you encounter an event featuring the Guest. Visiting the same Guest a second time in the Food Court gives a chibi date and triggers a second event later. Note: Guests have their own pepper count for their ending. To access a Guest ending you must also choose "None of them" during the Final Choice instead of a Romance Option. Image: a "Friend-O-Mat" call-a-friend screen shows guest portraits labeled Miranda, Scott, Vera, Yang Yang, Bruce, Gerard, Kale, Sawyer, and Susanne, plus locked slots. A black note overlays the lower right.
21=Page 22, Comic Stage. Text: There is a secondary quest: making your own comic. First choose a comic genre, each tied to a comic and art style; more genres unlock over runs. Each comic has two halves: a cover with plot summary and a page with panels. There are three Comic stages; in each you answer two questions, for six total. Each question has four answers. They affect the plot summary text and the panels, a bit like Mad Libs. Each option is tied to a stat; you gain the stat for the chosen option and lose the stats for unchosen options. Note: with several players, comic stat effects affect all players, so discussing options as a group is suggested. Image: three numbered panels show a genre selection grid, a comic-event dialogue screenshot, and a four-choice question screen.

22=Page 23, Mystery Shop. Text: Once per run, you visit the Mystery Shop and receive one free figurine. Figurines can have many potential effects. They are divided among Mystery Boxes with different themes and effect types. First choose a box; then, before discovering which figurine is inside, choose whether the figurine is for yourself or a gift for another player. Image: three numbered screenshots show a shop shelf of boxes and a white fox-like shopkeeper, a recipient choice panel reading "This item is for..." with options Myself and Someone else, and an item result popup for Green reading "No Smarts Smith" and "You lose a lot of ALL your stats," with an OK button.
23=Page 24, Mystery Shop box explanation. Text: "Dull Daniel Does Nothing" guarantees no effect. "Landmarks in Love" has stat effects tied to Locations. "Stats... But Now They're People!" has stat effects that are either slight or simple. "Zodiac Thirst: Cosmic Office" has slightly bigger stat effects. "Buff Bugs" has stat effects that are way more impactful or chaotic. "My Weird Little Village" changes the appearance of the Player Character or the Romance Options. "Major Arcana: No Mercy" changes visual or sound aspects of the game. Note: if you are not ready to risk a negative effect, consider the No Effect box. Image: seven illustrated boxes are arranged around the page, each with a pink label.
24=Page 25, Final Choice and Endings. Text: At the end of each run, all players make a final choice: who to ask to the con afterparty. You can choose one of the six Romance Options or the seventh option, None of Them. After that, you access one of several endings. Endings are divided into Failure Ending, Date Ending, Guest Date Ending, and Secret Ending. Image: four sample ending thumbnails: a purple failure scene with Liam and a flying bat, a bright romantic date image with April, a guest date image with a grey-haired character and another guest, and a secret ending image with Zoe among orange cat-like figures.
25=Page 26, Final Choice and Endings details. Text: Failure endings happen if you do not meet the requirements of your final choice; there are failures for each Romance Option and for None of Them. Date, guest date, and secret endings are considered good endings. Choosing None of Them can give a good alone ending. Completing a plotline tied to a Romance Option requires choosing that Romance Option for the Secret Ending, though some plotlines show several Romance Options while still being tied to one. Completing a plotline not tied to a Romance Option requires choosing None of Them. Completing Guest requirements requires choosing None of Them. Some date endings are platonic; each Romance Option has a platonic date ending, some Guests have platonic endings, and a successful alone date is platonic. Platonic endings use a blue frame instead of the red frame used by romantic and naughty endings.
26=Page 27, Plotlines. Text: Plotlines mostly have three events. The first two work like normal events. The third uses a Stat Threshold system instead of Stat Exchange. In a Stat Threshold event, each option is tied to a stat as usual, but each option has both a success outcome and a failure outcome. To get success, choose the option tied to your highest stat of the two. Image: a screenshot shows April beside a large image of a painted egg-like face, with two dialogue choices. The visible blue choice begins "Explain to April that this isn't creepy..." and the black choice begins "Explain to April that you could never abandon..."
27=Page 28, Settings and Customize. Text: Trigger Warnings help filter content you may want to avoid. Block completely avoids the content; Notify does not filter it but adds a warning icon. Most Trigger Warnings focus on stronger-than-usual instances. The sex trigger warning covers content that feels too explicit; the game remains generally horny, so some sexual content is not included. The same applies to violence. Note: Trigger Warnings do not affect plotlines or endings; pepper count controls ending horniness. A special Trigger Warning for creepy visuals affects visuals only, not text. Trigger Seen Plotlines Randomly disables random plotline repeats after they have been unlocked; this may reduce surprise but increases agency. Image: a purple settings screen shows Trigger Warning Filters for Drugs, Toilet Humor, Too Graphic, and Highly Violent, all set to Allow; other toggles include Replace Creepypasta Content, Trigger Seen Plotlines Randomly, and Allow Alternative Costumes. Buttons read Check Info Manual and View Full Credits.
28=Page 29, Pause Menu. Text: The pause menu lets you access settings, change the selected mixtape, and check character info such as Stats and Likes. Title: your character displays a title selected based on high stat, low stat, or specific events/options; titles have no function except flavor. Image: a pause screen shows player Blue, title "The Brain," stats Smarts 13, Boldness 10, Creativity 7, Charm 11, Fun 8, Likes including Owning You're a Clown, Roleplaying in Bed, and Correcting Other People, Top 3 icons, and menu buttons Resume, History, Settings, Back to Menu.
29=Page 30, Unlockables. Text: The game includes unlockables to reward repeated runs; a popup appears whenever you unlock something new. Unlockable categories: Plotlines, Outfits, Figurines, Comic Genres, Guests, Conversations, Mystery Boxes, and Gallery Content. Image: a dark screenshot shows Zoe sitting at a desk with a popup reading "Unlocked! You've just unlocked a new plotline!" and an OK button.
